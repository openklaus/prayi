Spiritual Prayer.

You do not need a tradition to pray. The impulse toward prayer — the reaching toward something larger than oneself, the desire to be heard, the act of naming what you carry — is one of the oldest and most universal human experiences. Spiritual prayer, in the non-denominational sense, honours that impulse without requiring doctrine or creed. It asks only for honesty: what is true right now, in this body, in this life?

Many people find themselves between traditions — raised in one faith, drawn to others, or simply living without a defined framework while still feeling the need to pray. This is not a problem to be solved. It is a perfectly valid place to stand. Spiritual prayer makes room for uncertainty, for wonder, and for the kind of gratitude that has nowhere particular to go. These words are for that space. Bring what you have. That is enough.

Example prayers

I do not know what to call You — or whether You have a name. But I know the feeling of needing to speak and having nowhere else to turn. So I turn here. May what I carry today be lighter by morning. May I be of some use to someone. That is all.
Thank you for the ordinary things: for this breath, this hour, this particular light. For the people who stayed. For the difficulties that did not break me, even when they tried. May I hold what I have been given with more care. May I give more freely than I take.
