Buddhist Prayer.

Buddhist prayer is, at its core, the cultivation of intention. Metta — loving-kindness — is perhaps the most central of Buddhist prayer forms: a deliberate extension of goodwill toward oneself, toward loved ones, toward strangers, and ultimately toward all beings without exception. This is not passive wishing. It is a practice of actively shaping the heart, slowly eroding the barriers between self and other, until compassion becomes the default response to existence.

Unlike petitionary prayer in other traditions, Buddhist prayer does not address an external deity. It is more like a dedication — an offering of the fruits of one's practice toward the welfare of all. Still, the impulse that motivates it is identical to prayer in any tradition: the desire to be well, to do good, and to acknowledge that we are not alone in our suffering or our joy. These words are a beginning. The practice is the work of a lifetime.

Example prayers

May I be free from suffering. May I be free from fear. May I find peace in the impermanence of this moment, and may that peace extend outward — to those I love, to those I struggle with, to all beings in all directions, without exception and without end.
In this breath, I arrive. Not yesterday, not tomorrow — here, where the only work is presence. May I meet what arises with openness. May I let what passes go without grasping. May compassion be the ground I walk on and the direction I face.
